I

The world & the broken rulebook

Presenter: Daniele

D&D combat in 60 seconds

  • A party (3–6 heroes, levels 1–20) versus monsters run by the Dungeon Master.
  • Every monster carries a Challenge Rating (CR) — the official danger score, 0 to 30.
  • The rulebook's recipe (DMG p.82): sum monster XP, multiply for monster count, compare with per-level thresholds.
The official promiseA CR-5 monster ≈ a fair fight for four level-5 heroes.
The realityCR is a design intention — never validated against actual play. Nobody had checked. So we did.

Our question is an estimand, not a score

Target η(x) = P(party wins | encounter x), calibrated — then inverted: the True Lethality Level is the party level where a balanced party of 4 reaches the target win rate (default 65%), by binary search on η.
  • Everything downstream consumes probabilities → calibration is a first-class requirement.
  • Boundary honesty instead of fake precision: trivial (≥ target even at level 1) · beyond deadly (< target even at level 20).

The Attack Potency problem the bestiary lists HP and AC — nothing about how hard a monster hits

  • We parse offense from the statblock text itself: +9 to hit, 12 (2d6+5), “makes three attacks”, DC 14, spell lists.
  • Per-action segmentation separates sustained DPR from burst — the single scariest action.

III

The model & honest evaluation

Presenter: NOME 3

Gradient boosting with domain priors

  • XGBoost, 45 features, Optuna-tuned under grouped CV: 300 trees, depth 4, heavy subsampling — shallow, as noisy logs demand.
  • 45 monotone constraints as shape priors: more party level can never hurt, more monster DPR can never help.
  • Inputs clipped to legal 5e ranges → graceful out-of-distribution behavior.
Serving pipelineFeatureEngineer → Imputer → XGBoost → Platt calibration. One pickle; the feature math travels with the model.
Why Platt, not isotonicIsotonic's step function collapsed the level search — 1 Lich ≡ 2 Liches. Sigmoid is smooth and measured better (Brier 0.1394 vs 0.1397).

  • Encounters in one campaign share party, DM, house rules ⇒ random splits leak. StratifiedGroupKFold everywhere.
  • The grouped-CV vs naive-split gap is the leakage — we report it instead of hiding it.
  • AUC ≈ 0.66 is honest, not weak: dice, tactics and unrecorded items are irreducible noise, and the base rate is 83%.

A model that wins AUC and still loses the most instructive result of the whole project

  • Ridge logistic beats XGBoost on observational risk: grouped-CV AUC 0.655 vs 0.614.
  • Promoted to production, it rated 8 Liches as trivial and a 10,000-HP monster as beatable.
  • The coefficients confess: num_monsters, total_dpr, burst all positive; avg_party_level negative — “stronger parties lose more”, because stronger parties are served harder content. Selection confounding, visible to the naked eye.
LessonThe app asks interventional questions. Prediction ≠ decision.

The course toolbox, where each tool fits identical campaign-grouped CV for every contender

  • MMD two-sample test (kernel, Lec 06): train vs held-out campaign features — MMD² = 0.00031, permutation p = 0.12 → no covariate shift; the CV-holdout gap is outcome variance. The split design holds.
  • Gaussian Process CR predictor (n = 797): MAE 0.86 vs XGBoost 0.94, R² 0.954 — with 95% intervals at 0.95 empirical coverage.
  • GAN ablation: CTGAN class balancing → AUC 0.638 / Brier 0.186 vs real-data 0.657 / 0.139. Resampling destroys calibration to fix a non-problem.
IV

Where the data lies:
guards & simulation

Presenter: NOME 4

A quiz for the room a balanced party of four level-8 heroes meets NINETEEN CR-21 Liches. Their win chance?

≈ 0% — obviously hopeless
≈ 15% — a miracle could happen
66% — “a fair fight”
Our early model said 66%. The bug wasn't in the code — it was in the world. In the logs, fights the damage math calls hopeless (party deleted in ≤ 1 round) were still “won” 84.5% of the time. Fudged rolls, retreats recorded as wins, reinforcements: DM mercy. The data answers “what happens at real tables” — the app asks “what if you fought to the death”. No amount of training data fixes an estimand mismatch.

Three guards between the model and the user

  1. Shape priors + clipping — monotone constraints, legal-range inputs: absurd homebrew degrades gracefully.
  2. Roster dominance — averages dilute (Lich + 6 goblins “looks easier” than the Lich alone): score every homogeneous sub-roster, keep the minimum. Adding monsters can never help the party.
  3. Survival physics cap — P(win) ≤ σ(a·TTK + b), TTK = deterministic rounds-to-kill-the-party. Anchored by judgment on day one…
…and a hand-tuned constant is a confession. So we bought 490,640 simulated battles and made the simulator set it.

The guard, calibrated on deathmatch truth 180 Battlecast cells · 2,000 Monte Carlo trials each · drag the slider

2.0 cap now:33% hand-tuned said:50%

Binomial-weighted logistic fit: σ(1.63·TTK − 3.98) replaces σ(2.197·TTK − 4.394). The hand guess was too generous exactly in the 2–3-round zone; inert above ≈ 3.7 rounds, where training data is trustworthy.

Limits — stated, not hidden

  • Observational ceiling: AUC ≈ 0.66 in an 83%-win world — the model ranks well; it is no oracle.
  • Estimand mismatch handled by an explicit, sim-calibrated guard — a documented modeling choice, not something learned end-to-end.
  • Simulator caveats: 2024-SRD rules vs 2014-era logs; scripted optimal play ⇒ deathmatch numbers are an upper bound.
  • Thin support on hard fights — future work: VTT instrumentation, post-encounter forms with explicit retreat/fudge labels, an all-SRD replay grid.

Five takeaways

  1. Validate by the unit of leakage — or every number is fiction.
  2. Calibrate when the product consumes probabilities; measure it, don't assume it.
  3. Prediction ≠ decision — a better-AUC model can be unusable; constraints encode the difference.
  4. Know your estimand — observational data can't answer counterfactuals; simulation can, and can calibrate your priors.
  5. The rulebook deserved a fair trial — honestly implemented, it still loses to 34,907 real fights.
