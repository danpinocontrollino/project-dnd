I

The world & the broken rulebook

Presenter: Daniele

D&D combat in 60 seconds

  • A party (3–6 heroes, levels 1–20) versus monsters run by the Dungeon Master. Combat runs in rounds: initiative order, one action + movement each: action economy matter more than any single statblock.
  • Every monster carries a Challenge Rating (CR), the official danger score, 0 to 30, printed once and never audited.
  • The rulebook's recipe (DMG p.82): sum monster XP, multiply for monster count, compare with per-level thresholds → Easy / Medium / Hard / Deadly.
The four roles our model sees (from the class logs)
HealerCleric · Druid · Bard — turns attrition wars around TankBarbarian · Fighter · Paladin — soaks the frontline ArcaneWizard · Sorcerer · Warlock — range & control, the answer to flyers Martial DPSRogue · Monk · Ranger — sustained damage, halved by physical resistance
The official promiseA CR-5 monster ≈ a fair fight for four level-5 heroes. The DMG's encounter tables (p.82) turn CR into XP budgets and grade every fight Easy → Deadly. One number is supposed to summarize offense, defense, traits and action economy.
The realityCR is a design intention and it's never validated against actual play. Known failure modes every DM meets: high-CR solos that fold to action economy, low-CR swarms that kill, trait synergies (a flyer vs a melee party) invisible to a single score. Those are not measured in the manuals, so we tried!
The cast of this talk

Relevant stats — HP (how much damage it absorbs) · AC (how hard it is to hit: d20 + bonus ≥ AC) · DPR (average damage per round) · burst (its single scariest action) · save DC (how hard its effects are to resist).

Our question is an estimand, not a score

Target η(x) = P(party wins | encounter x), calibrated, then inverted: the True Lethality Level is the party level where a balanced party of 4 reaches the target win rate (default 65%), by binary search on η.
  • Everything downstream consumes probabilities → calibration is a first-class requirement, measured (Brier, reliability curves), never assumed.
  • The search: binary search on party level 1–20 (18 iterations, answer snapped to quarter levels), against a fixed reference (a balanced party of four).
🕊️ trivial
P(win) ≥ target already at level 1. Real tables reliably beat this at any level, no fractional level is invented.

Seen in the wild: Goblin (P(win) at level 1 ≈ 87%), Ogre, a pack of six CR-1 wolves. The 83% base rate makes weak monsters saturate near the ceiling for every level, so the app explains the saturation instead of printing a meaningless “level 0.25”.

This verdict is itself a finding: at real tables, the danger of weak monsters is attrition across a day, never the single fight.
⚔️ level L
The target is crossed inside [1, 20]: L is the True Lethality Level, reported with the exact win probability at that level.

Seen in the wild: 1 Lich → 3.25 · 2 Liches → 6.75 · 4 Liches → 12.75. Official CR for the Lich says 21: the single biggest book-vs-data discrepancy we measured.

We report “level 12.75 (64.6% win at this level)” because two encounters can share a level and still differ in risk, so the probability is the product and the level is the summary.
☠️ beyond deadly
P(win) < target even at level 20. A TPK machine and the app says so instead of extrapolating.

Seen in the wild: the Tarrasque; 8+ Liches; any homebrew past the physics guard (19 Liches: P(win) at level 20 = 9.1%).

The verdict ships with its ceiling (“even a level-20 party only wins X%”) so the DM knows how far beyond deadly, without us inventing a fantasy “level 27” the model has no support for.

Why 65%? It is a choice: the app exposes it as a slider (0.50–0.90). Real tables win 83% of fights, so ~0.85 ≈ “typical curated encounter” and 0.55 ≈ “a true coin flip”.

The Attack Potency problem the bestiary lists HP and AC — nothing about how hard a monster hits

  • We parse offense from the statblock text itself: +9 to hit, 12 (2d6+5), “makes three attacks”, DC 14, spell lists.
  • Per-action segmentation separates sustained DPR from burst: the single scariest action.
  • Coverage: 321/762 real statblocks; the rest imputed from the DMG design table by CR.
  • Regression tests caught real bugs: versatile weapons double-counted (24 monsters), dragon breath leaking into multiattack math (Adult Red 72.6 → 58 DPR).

III

The model & honest evaluation

Presenter: Stefano

Gradient boosting with domain priors

  • XGBoost, 45 features, Optuna-tuned under grouped CV: 300 trees, depth 4, heavy subsampling — shallow and regularized, exactly what noisy real-play logs demand.
  • 45 monotone constraints as shape priors baked into every split: party level, size and each role flag +; monster count, CR, HP, DPR, burst, attack bonus, save DC and every hostile trait −. Higher level can never hurt; more monster DPR can never help.
  • Inputs clipped to legal 5e bands before features: level 1–20, roster ≤ 30, AC 10–30, DPR ≤ 350 (≤ 1,500 pooled), burst ≤ 400, save DC 8–30 ⇒ a 10,000-HP homebrew lands on the manifold edge, not on a random leaf.
  • The constraints are also the OOD story: beyond the data, trees can only flatten in the safe direction — never invert.
Serving pipelineFeatureEngineer → Imputer → XGBoost → Platt calibration. One pickle; the feature math travels with the model.
Why Platt, not isotonicIsotonic's step function collapsed the level search — 1 Lich ≡ 2 Liches. Sigmoid is smooth and measured better (Brier 0.1394 vs 0.1397).
Chosen by Optuna (grouped-CV objective, study persisted)

  • Encounters in one campaign share party, DM, house rules ⇒ random splits leak. StratifiedGroupKFold everywhere — the split key is the campaign log, all 1,462 of them.
  • Even calibration is grouped: the Platt folds use precomputed campaign-aware splits, so the base model and its calibrator never peek at the same table.
  • Grouped CV says 0.601 ± 0.040; the grouped holdout says 0.657. The gap between grouped and naive numbers is the leakage — reported, not shipped.
  • CIs by percentile bootstrap: 2,000 resamples of the 7,106 held-out encounters, degenerate resamples skipped.
  • AUC ≈ 0.66 is honest, not weak: dice, tactics and unrecorded magic items are irreducible noise — and a blind “party always wins” baseline is already right 83% of the time.

A model that wins AUC and still loses the most instructive result of the whole project

  • Ridge logistic beats XGBoost on observational risk: grouped-CV AUC 0.655 vs 0.614.
  • Promoted to production, it rated 8 Liches as trivial and a 10,000-HP monster as beatable.
  • The coefficients confess: num_monsters, total_dpr, burst all positive; avg_party_level negative — “stronger parties lose more”, because stronger parties are served harder content. Selection confounding, visible to the naked eye.
LessonThe app asks interventional questions. Prediction ≠ decision.

The course toolbox, where each tool fits identical campaign-grouped CV for every contender

  • MMD two-sample test (kernel, Lec 06): train vs held-out campaign features — MMD² = 0.00031, permutation p = 0.12 → no covariate shift; the CV-holdout gap is outcome variance. The split design holds.
  • Gaussian Process CR predictor (n = 797): MAE 0.86 vs XGBoost 0.94, R² 0.954 — with 95% intervals at 0.95 empirical coverage.
  • GAN ablation: CTGAN class balancing → AUC 0.638 / Brier 0.186 vs real-data 0.657 / 0.139 — and the predicted base rate collapses to 0.61. Resampling destroys calibration to fix a problem proper losses never had.

Our early model said 66%. The bug wasn't in the code — it was in the world. In the logs, fights the damage math calls hopeless (party deleted in ≤ 1 round) were still “won” 84.5% of the time. Fudged rolls, retreats recorded as wins, reinforcements: DM mercy. The data answers “what happens at real tables” — the app asks “what if you fought to the death”. No amount of training data fixes an estimand mismatch.

Three guards between the model and the user

  1. Shape priors + clipping — 45 monotone constraints and legal-range clipping: absurd homebrew degrades gracefully. Caught in testing: a 10,000-HP, AC-50, DPR-999 monster → clean beyond deadly through the real app flow, no crash, no nonsense.
  2. Roster dominance — count-weighted averages dilute: Lich + 2 Ogres + 6 Goblins has avg CR 2.94 while the Lich alone is CR 21, so the raw model rated the bigger fight easier (level 3.25 vs 5.0). Fix: score every homogeneous sub-roster and keep the minimum P(win) — adding monsters can never help the party. Axiom, not heuristic.
  3. Survival physics cap — P(win) ≤ σ(a·TTK + b), TTK = deterministic rounds-to-kill-the-party from the same feature math the model trains on: smooth, monotone in level, decreasing in roster damage, so binary search and dominance both survive. Anchored by judgment on day one to 10% / 50% / 90% at 1 / 2 / 3 rounds…
…and a hand-tuned constant is a confession. So we bought 490,640 simulated battles and made the simulator set it.
With all three guards, the Lich ladder: 1× → level 3.25 2× → 6.75 4× → 12.75 8+× → ☠️ beyond deadly — monotone, dominance-safe, pinned by 61 tests + 13 axioms
Why guards, not more dataEach guard blocks a failure that no amount of the same data can fix: extrapolation (no data exists out there), aggregation dilution (an artifact of our own featurization), and estimand mismatch (the logs answer a different question). Diagnosis first, then the right tool.
Order of applicationClipping happens inside the feature transformer (so training and serving agree) → the model predicts → dominance takes the minimum over sub-rosters → the physics cap clamps the result. Deterministic, testable, and visible in predict_win_for_parties().

Limits — stated, not hidden

Observational ceiling
AUC 0.657 [0.638, 0.674], PR-AUC 0.874, log-loss 0.447 in an 83%-win world: dice variance, table tactics, homebrew magic items and DM style are irreducible noise the features cannot see. The model ranks encounters well; it is no oracle.

Naive random splits report far higher numbers — that gap is same-campaign leakage, and we show it instead of shipping it. Every headline metric carries a bootstrap 95% CI (2,000 resamples).
Estimand mismatch
“Fight to the death” is not in observational data: in the logs, fights the damage math calls hopeless were still “won” 84.5% of the time (659 rows of DM mercy).

Handled by an explicit cap, σ(1.63·TTK − 3.98) — fitted on the Battlecast guard grid, anchors 9% / 33% / 71% at 1 / 2 / 3 rounds of survival, inert above ≈ 3.7 where training data is trustworthy. A documented modeling choice with its own regression tests, not something silently learned.
Simulator caveats
Battlecast runs 2024-SRD rules (317 statblocks) while our logs are 2014-era play — several monsters were rebalanced between editions. Its heroes are optimized default builds with scripted play ⇒ deathmatch win rates are an upper bound on real-table performance.

The engine is vendored (three unmodified public assets) so the grid runs locally, reproducibly, with zero load on the service — full credits and caveats in battlecast_bridge/PROVENANCE.md.
Thin support on hard fights
Curated play rarely produces hopeless battles: 96 of 100 mercy-grid cells are deathmatch-easy, so the +0.32 mercy estimate rests on a handful of hard cells — directional, not precise.

Next steps, in order of value: a Foundry VTT module (~200 lines) exporting clean combat events; a 30-second post-encounter form adding explicit retreat/fudge labels — turning mercy from contamination into a censoring variable; an all-SRD replay grid re-fighting real logged encounters in the simulator.

Five takeaways

  1. Validate by the unit of leakage (the campaign) — or every number is fiction.
  2. Calibrate when the product consumes probabilities; measure it (Brier 0.139, curves), don't assume it.
  3. Prediction ≠ decision — the better-AUC logistic was unusable; monotone constraints encode the difference.
  4. Know your estimand — observational data can't answer counterfactuals; 490k simulated deathmatches can, and calibrated our prior.
  5. The rulebook deserved a fair trial — honestly implemented, it still loses to 34,907 real fights.
